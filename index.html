When a patient has more than one insurance plan, payer ORDER matters. Every concept gets an immediate check-in question.

Concept 1 — Coordination of Benefits

When a patient has two or more insurance plans, COB rules decide who pays first.

  • Primary payer — pays first, at full benefit, ignoring other coverage
  • Secondary payer — pays after primary; receives primary EOB with the claim
  • Tertiary — third in line (rare). Always bill in order — never simultaneously
  • Total payment across all plans cannot exceed 100% of the charge

Concept 2 — Critical COB Rules to Memorize

Who Pays First? The Non-Negotiable Rules

  • Medicaid = ALWAYS payer of last resort. Bill every other insurance first.
  • Medicare + Medicaid (dual eligible) = Medicare primary, Medicaid last
  • Auto accident injury = Auto/no-fault insurance is primary for accident-related medical bills
  • Work-related injury = Workers' compensation is always primary
  • Active employer plan vs. COBRA = Active employer plan is primary

Concept 3 — The Birthday Rule

For Dependent Children Covered Under Both Parents' Plans

  • The parent whose birthday falls EARLIEST in the calendar year has the primary plan
  • It is month and day ONLY — the YEAR of birth does NOT matter
  • Example: Mom = March 15, Dad = July 4 → Mom's plan is primary (March < July)
  • If birthdays are the same date → the plan in effect longer is primary
  • This rule applies to dependents — not the subscriber themselves

💵 Patient Financial Responsibility

Five terms. Every one gets taught — then tested — before moving on.

Concept 4 — Premium

Premium = Monthly Cost to Keep the Policy Active

  • Paid to the insurer every month regardless of whether services are used
  • Think of it as a subscription fee for having coverage
  • NOT collected by the medical office during a visit
  • Does NOT count toward the deductible or out-of-pocket maximum

Concept 5 — Deductible

Deductible = Patient Pays First, Before Insurance Kicks In

  • The annual amount the patient must pay out-of-pocket before the insurance begins paying (for most services)
  • Resets every plan year
  • Critical billing check: Has the deductible been met? How much remains?
  • If $800 of a $1,000 deductible is met → patient still owes $200 before insurance pays

Concept 6 — Copay vs. Coinsurance

Two Very Different Things

  • Copay = a FIXED dollar amount per visit (e.g., $30 every office visit). Collect at time of service. Does not vary with the service cost.
  • Coinsurance = a PERCENTAGE of the allowed amount AFTER the deductible is met (e.g., patient pays 20%, insurance pays 80%). Varies based on the service cost.
  • Medicare Part B = 20% coinsurance after deductible (unless Medigap covers it)
  • A plan can have BOTH — copay for office visits AND coinsurance for procedures
Check-In — Copay vs Coinsurance · True or False?

Concept 7 — Out-of-Pocket Maximum

OOP Max = The Patient's Annual Cost Ceiling

  • The most the patient will pay in a plan year for covered services
  • Once the OOP max is reached → insurance pays 100% for covered services for the rest of the year
  • Premium payments do NOT count toward OOP max
  • Non-covered services still remain the patient's responsibility
  • Billing check: If OOP max is met → patient owes $0 copay, $0 coinsurance for covered services

Concept 8 — The 6 Most Common Denial Causes

All preventable. All front-end failures.

🏷️
Wrong Payer Assumption
Treating all plans of the same type the same. E.g., assuming all PPOs have no prior auth requirements.
✅ Fix: Verify THIS plan's specific rules, not just the plan type.
📤
Missed Referral
HMO or POS patient sees specialist without a PCP referral on file.
✅ Fix: Verify referral BEFORE the specialist appointment is confirmed.
🔒
Missing Prior Auth
High-cost procedure rendered without required preauthorization.
✅ Fix: Check auth requirements during eligibility verification, obtain BEFORE service.
🌐
Network Error
Service rendered by or at an out-of-network provider without informing the patient.
✅ Fix: Verify provider AND facility network status for the patient's specific plan.
📋
Eligibility Not Verified
Assuming coverage based on a card or prior visit — especially dangerous for Medicaid.
✅ Fix: Verify eligibility for every date of service, every visit.
🔄
Wrong COB Order
Billing Medicaid first, or billing secondary before primary EOB is received.
✅ Fix: Identify all coverage, apply COB rules, bill in correct order.

Concept 9 — What Went Wrong? Denial Cases

Open each case. Identify the root cause before revealing it.

Situation: A patient with an HMO called to schedule a dermatology appointment. The scheduler confirmed insurance, verified copay, and booked the visit. The dermatologist saw the patient. Claim denied: "Referral required — not on file."


Root cause: The scheduler verified insurance eligibility but did NOT check whether an HMO referral from the PCP existed. No referral = automatic denial for an HMO specialist claim.


What should have happened: Step 1 → Confirm plan type is HMO. Step 2 → Check if specialist visits require referral (yes, always for HMO). Step 3 → Confirm PCP referral exists. Step 4 → If not, patient must get referral before appointment is scheduled.

💰 Revenue lesson: A $0 referral check prevents hours of denial work and a potential write-off.

Situation: A Medicaid patient was seen on the 12th of the month. Office had verified Medicaid eligibility 6 weeks prior and assumed it was still active. Claim denied: "Patient not eligible on date of service."


Root cause: Medicaid eligibility was not verified for the current date of service. The patient's Medicaid had lapsed due to an income change between the prior visit and this one.


What should have happened: Verify Medicaid eligibility on or right before EVERY DATE OF SERVICE — not just at intake. Use payer portal or clearinghouse for real-time check.

Situation: A dual-eligible patient (Medicare + Medicaid) received services. The biller submitted the claim to Medicaid first. Medicaid denied: "Medicare is primary — bill Medicare first."


Root cause: The biller did not apply COB rules. Medicaid = payer of last resort. For dual-eligible patients, Medicare is ALWAYS billed first.


What should have happened: Identify dual-eligible status during intake. Apply COB rule: Medicare primary → receive EOB → bill Medicaid with EOB attached.

Medicare Parts

Part AHospital Insurance — inpatient, SNF, hospice. Bill when ADMITTED.
Part BMedical/Outpatient — physician, lab, DME, imaging. Pays 80%; patient owes 20%.
Part CMedicare Advantage — REPLACES Original Medicare. Bill the private plan, NOT CMS.
Part DPrescription Drugs — pharmacy only. Bill the PDP plan. In-office drugs = Part B.
MedigapPrivate supplement for Original Medicare gaps. Fills 20% coinsurance. NOT Part C.
Part C ≠ MedigapPart C REPLACES. Medigap SUPPLEMENTS. Different workflows entirely.
